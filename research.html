Child development and the production of noncognitive skills

Abstract: This article develops a dynamic model of the production of children’s cognitive and noncognitive skills, extending the identification strategy developed by Agostinelli and Wiswall (2025). Skill formation depends on the existing stock of skills and parental investment, which in turn is shaped by various household characteristics. The model explicitly accounts for measurement error in skill and investment variables. Children’s skill levels in the final period are used to predict adult outcomes. The estimated production functions show that the mother’s and child’s noncognitive skills, along with household income, are the key determinants of parental investment. Cognitive skill formation is primarily driven by the child’s prior cognitive skills and parental investment, whereas noncognitive skill formation depends almost exclusively on the child’s previous stock of noncognitive skills. Finally, noncognitive skills in the last period are found to be stronger predictors of adult outcomes than cognitive skills

Marriage market and child decision making: a matching model

Abstract: I develop a many-to-one matching model with transferable utility in which men are characterized by an income variable (continuous) and women by both income and preferences regarding childbearing (binary). The model incorporates women’s decisions over fertility and participation in the workforce. I characterize the equilibrium matching patterns and intra-household allocations. The model predicts a non-assortative equilibrium in which high income men marry lower income women who specialize in child caretaking. On the other hand, higher income women with greater child caretaking productivity are able to both work and have children.

The empirics of a marriage market model with children

Abstract: This paper tests the predictions of a marriage market model using data from the NLSY79 for women born between 1957 and 1964. Using data on income, education, marital status, and fertility measured when women are 28 years old, I find that the data support the predictions of the model. I find positive assortative matching on income conditional on both spouses working, as measured by the SEV index proposed by Chiappori et al. (2020). However, when considering the full dataset, there is no assortativeness, as the highest income men tend to marry lower income women, consistent with the model’s prediction of specialization in child-rearing.
